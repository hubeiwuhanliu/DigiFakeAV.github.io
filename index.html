Abstract

Overview

In recent years, deepfake technology has advanced rapidly, yet its misuse poses serious threats to information security and public safety. Existing datasets have mainly focused on traditional face-swapping techniques, failing to reflect the emerging trend of digital human generation methods. These diffusion-based approaches can generate highly realistic videos from speech and target images, offering greater flexibility, stealthiness, and multimodal coherence, thus challenging current detection strategies.


To address this issue, we introduce DigiFakeAV, the first large-scale multimodal deepfake dataset based on digital human synthesis. It contains 60,000 videos (8.4 million frames) with diverse identities across nationalities, skin tones, and genders. Experimental results show that detection models suffer over 30% performance drop on DigiFakeAV, and user studies confirm that the fake videos are nearly indistinguishable from real ones.

Methodology

DigiFakeAV Pipeline

We generate DigiFakeAV using five recent digital human generation methods (Sonic, Hallo1, Hallo2, Echomimic, V-Express) and one advanced audio synthesis approach (CosyVoice 2). Compared to prior deepfake dataset generation techniques, our selection offers notable differences and advantages in realism and multimodal coherence.

Samples

Dataset Visualizations

FV-RA

Fake Video – Real Audio

This category consists of fake videos created by synthesizing visual content conditioned on authentic audio. We extract audio from real videos and convert it to WAV format. RetinaFace detects and crops representative facial frames. Using five digital human generation techniques — Sonic, Hallo1, Hallo2, Echomimic, V-Express — we produce a total of 25,000 forged videos. Compared to traditional face-swapping methods, these forgeries pose a more credible and serious threat.

FV-FA

Fake Video – Fake Audio

This category encompasses both manipulated audio and video. We employ state-of-the-art voice cloning (CosyVoice 2): first generating manipulated text via a large language model, then synthesizing fake audio with the target speaker's vocal characteristics. Using Sonic, Hallo1, Hallo2, and Echomimic, we generate 25,000 forged videos. This approach offers fraudsters a more flexible and realistic means of deception.

Roadmap

Future Works

🚀

Expanding Synthesis Methods

Recent advances in digital human generation have led to increasingly realistic forgery techniques that surpass existing methods such as Sonic. We plan to continuously integrate emerging methods to keep the benchmark at the frontier.

🌐

Diverse Real-World Scenarios

We aim to expand DigiFakeAV with more diverse real-world scenarios — including different lighting conditions, languages, and speaking styles — to improve dataset comprehensiveness and representativeness.

🔬

Robust Detection Research

These developments pose growing threats to multimedia security and social trust. We aim to foster novel research directions ensuring detection models remain effective against increasingly sophisticated deepfake attacks.
